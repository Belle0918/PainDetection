\begin{abstract}
Self-reported pain scales do not work for patients who cannot communicate and cannot be used for continuous monitoring, so there is interest in inferring pain from physiological signals. We study this problem on the PainMonit Database (N=101), which contains both a controlled experimental heat-pain subset (PMED) and a clinical physiotherapy subset (PMCD). On the same subject-independent protocol we compare classical feature-based classifiers (RF / SVM), an early-fusion CNN, an attention-fusion model, a CrossMod-Transformer adapted from \citet{farmani2025crossmod}, a PMED$\to$PMCD transfer pipeline, and four LLM-based setups on Qwen3-4B and Phi-4-mini (zero-shot prompting, supervised fine-tuning with CoT, GRPO/SRPO reinforcement learning, and a frozen-backbone classification head). Which model wins depends on the data regime. On clinical PMCD, the 4.6M-parameter CrossMod-Transformer reaches Macro F1 = 0.594, beating every other model including the 4B-parameter LLM. On experimental PMED, the Qwen3-4B classification head reaches Macro F1 = 0.649 and AUC = 0.940, well ahead of every specialised fusion model. Cross-context transfer is weaker than in-domain training for every method we tried: RF collapses to 0.366 F1, and even the LLM drops by about 0.05 F1 from PMED$\to$PMCD. The same LLM classification head also beats generative SFT / GRPO / SRPO on the same backbone, and Phi-4-mini collapses on PMCD under an otherwise identical pipeline. Taken together, these results point to a trade-off between narrow inductive bias and large-scale priors that depends on the label regime and on how much inter-subject variability the data contains.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Pain is subjective by definition, which makes it hard to measure objectively. Clinical practice still relies on self-reported scales such as the Numerical Rating Scale (NRS), but these do not work for patients with impaired communication, cognitive limitations, or sedation~\citep{finley1998measurement}, and they only give intermittent snapshots rather than continuous monitoring. This has motivated a line of work on automated pain recognition from behavioural and physiological signals~\citep{gruss2015pain,walter2013biovid,werner2019twofold}.

Most existing work studies pain recognition within a single setting: either controlled lab experiments~\citep{walter2013biovid,gruss2019multimodal} or clinical recordings~\citep{gouverneur2024painmonit}. Whether representations learned under clean conditions carry over to noisy clinical data is an open question. The PainMonit Database~\citep{gouverneur2024painmonit} is a good testbed because it releases both types of data under the same sensor setup: PMED contains experimentally induced heat pain with high-quality labels, while PMCD contains physiotherapy recordings with weaker labels, missing channels, and much more physiological variability.

In this paper we evaluate six model families on PainMonit under a common subject-independent protocol: classical feature-based classifiers (RF / SVM), an early-fusion 1D-CNN, an attention-fusion network with per-modality encoders, a CrossMod-Transformer adapted from \citet{farmani2025crossmod}, a PMED$\to$PMCD transfer pipeline, and four LLM-based setups on Qwen3-4B and Phi-4-mini. The LLM group covers zero-shot prompting, LoRA supervised fine-tuning with chain-of-thought targets, GRPO and SRPO reinforcement learning on top of SFT, and a frozen-backbone classification head that replaces text generation with a linear layer on top of the final hidden state.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item A unified subject-independent benchmark on PainMonit that covers classical, neural-fusion, transformer-fusion, transfer-learning, and LLM methods on both PMED and PMCD.
    \item Evidence that the best model depends on the subset: CrossMod-Transformer wins on PMCD (Macro F1 = 0.594) at roughly $1{,}000\times$ fewer parameters than the LLM, while Qwen3-4B + classification head wins on PMED (Macro F1 = 0.649, AUC = 0.940).
    \item A controlled comparison of four LLM adaptation strategies on the same backbone: the classification head beats generative SFT, GRPO, and SRPO on every metric we tracked, and also removes parse failures and gives calibrated probabilities.
    \item An error analysis showing that CrossMod and the LLM fail on PMCD in mirrored ways (CrossMod collapses Severe into Moderate; the LLM over-triggers Severe), which implies that class-imbalance fixes have to be picked per architecture.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Automated Pain Recognition}

Automated pain assessment has moved from hand-engineered features with classical classifiers such as SVMs and Random Forests~\citep{gruss2015pain,breiman2001random} toward end-to-end deep models. CNNs and LSTMs~\citep{hochreiter1997lstm} have been applied to facial action units~\citep{rodriguez2017deep}, physiological time series~\citep{thiam2019exploring,kessler2019pain}, and multimodal fusion of video, audio, and biosignals~\citep{salekin2019multichannel,werner2019twofold}.

\citet{wang2019recurrent} combined surface EMG and body movement using recurrent neural networks for detecting chronic pain protective behavior. \citet{werner2019twofold} introduced the first approach addressing multimodality in both sensor inputs and stimulation types. \citet{lopez2017multi} proposed multi-task neural networks for personalized pain recognition from physiological signals. More recently, \citet{farmani2025crossmod} proposed the CrossMod-Transformer, a hierarchical fusion architecture with per-modality FCN + attention-LSTM encoders and bidirectional cross-attention, reaching state-of-the-art results on experimental pain datasets using EDA and ECG. Most prior studies, however, evaluate within a single data context and do not assess whether learned representations transfer across experimental and clinical settings.

\subsection{Pain Databases}

Several multimodal pain databases have been developed. The BioVid Heat Pain Database~\citep{walter2013biovid} provided synchronized video and biosignals across thermal pain intensities. The SenseEmotion Database~\citep{velana2016senseemotion} expanded the scope to body movement and facial expressions. The X-ITE Database~\citep{gruss2019multimodal} further introduced thermal and electrical pain with synchronized audio. However, all of these datasets capture only laboratory-induced pain.

The PainMonit Database~\citep{gouverneur2024painmonit} is distinctive in that it provides both experimental (PMED) and clinical (PMCD) physiological recordings under a single sensor setup, which lets us study cross-context transfer without confounding dataset changes with sensor changes.

\subsection{Cross-Context Generalization}

Generalisation across data-collection settings is a central open problem in automated pain recognition. Models developed on laboratory datasets benefit from clean supervision and stable signal quality, while clinical data introduce weaker labels, missing channels, and broader physiological variability. PainMonit~\citep{gouverneur2024painmonit} lets us evaluate across both contexts on the same sensor setup. Our work builds on this by asking not only how well each model performs in-domain, but whether representations learned on the experimental subset remain useful on the clinical subset, and whether the general-purpose priors of a large LLM transfer better than the narrower inductive bias of a specialised fusion model.

\subsection{LLMs for Clinical and Tabular Data}

Large language models have been applied to clinical reasoning tasks~\citep{singhal2023medpalm,nori2023gpt4medicine}, and recent work shows that tabular and time-series data can be serialised into text for LLM-based classification~\citep{hegselmann2023tabllm,liu2024timellm}. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods such as LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} make it feasible to adapt 4B-parameter backbones on a single consumer GPU. Our LLM experiments build on this line of work. We serialise each window as structured text with subject-level z-scored features (including HRV, SCR count, EMG burst count, and respiration-rate variability), and compare four adaptation strategies on the same backbone: supervised fine-tuning with chain-of-thought targets, GRPO, SRPO, and a frozen-backbone classification-head setup that skips generation.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}
%% ============================================================

We use the PainMonit Database, a publicly available physiological signal dataset for automated pain recognition~\citep{gouverneur2024painmonit}. It contains two complementary subsets spanning 101 participants total.

\paragraph{PMED (Experimental).} The experimental subset (PMED) contains recordings from healthy participants subjected to controlled heat pain stimuli at graded intensities. Although the original data descriptor \citet{gouverneur2024painmonit} reports a cohort of 55 subjects, the officially released version of the dataset includes 52 participants (N=52). In this work, we conduct our analysis on these 52 available subjects to ensure reproducibility and alignment with the public repository. Physiological signals include blood volume pulse (BVP), electrodermal activity (EDA from two devices: Empatica E4 and RespiBAN), skin temperature, ECG, EMG, and respiration, originally recorded at 500 Hz and resampled to 250 Hz. Continuous pain ratings are provided via the Computerized Visual Analog Scale (CoVAS). Each temperature level is applied eight times per subject; 10-second stimulus-aligned epochs are extracted, with baseline epochs drawn from a 10-second window ending 5 seconds before stimulus onset.

\paragraph{PMCD (Clinical).} Recordings from 49 patients during physiotherapy sessions. Signals include BVP (two devices), EDA (two devices), EMG, respiration, and skin temperature. Pain labels are derived from discrete NRS self-reports mapped to 4-second windows with 50\% overlap. The label distribution is heavily skewed: no-pain 1443, moderate 1527, severe 485 segments (14\% severe), motivating the use of class-weighted loss and focal loss in our experiments.

\paragraph{Label scheme.}
We adopt a unified three-class scheme \emph{no-pain}, \emph{moderate}, and \emph{severe} across both subsets. Binary classification (pain vs.\ no-pain) is insufficient for clinical decision-making: a patient reporting mild discomfort and one in severe distress both receive a positive label, yet they require fundamentally different interventions. A three-class scheme better reflects the clinically actionable question of how much pain is present, not merely whether it exists.

PMED stimuli are calibrated per subject: the pain threshold $T_P$ (first temperature perceived as painful) and tolerance $T_T$ (first temperature reported as unbearable) are found by a staircase procedure~\citep{gouverneur2024painmonit}. A baseline $B$ is recorded at resting skin temperature ($32^{\circ}$C), a warm sub-threshold stimulus $\text{NP} = T_P - (T_T - T_P)/4$ is used as a no-pain control, and four painful stimuli are defined as $P_i = T_P + i \cdot (T_T - T_P)/4$ for $i \in \{1,2,3,4\}$, giving six stimulus classes in total ($B$, NP, $P_1$--$P_4$). Our default stimulus-based PMED mapping is $\{B, \text{NP}\}\to$ no-pain, $\{P_1, P_2\}\to$ moderate, $\{P_3, P_4\}\to$ severe. Because these bins split physical intensity rather than a clinically validated severity cutoff, we additionally report a CoVAS-based mapping from the subjective rating quartiles: $Q_0 \to$ no-pain, $Q_{1,2} \to$ moderate, $Q_{3,4} \to$ severe. PMCD labels are already three-class through the per-subject NRS thresholds released by the dataset authors.

\paragraph{Modality Selection.}
For the deep-learning experiments we use the four modalities that are shared between PMED and PMCD: Blood Volume Pulse (BVP), Electrodermal Activity (EDA), Electromyography (EMG), and respiration ($M=4$). For the classical baselines (RF, SVM) we additionally include the RespiBAN EDA channel (\textit{Eda\_RB}), since feature-based models do not need a fixed encoder architecture for cross-domain transfer; this gives $M=5$ for those runs. For EDA we use the \textit{Empatica E4} wrist channel by default, because wrist skin has higher sweat gland density than the chest-mounted \textit{RespiBAN} site.

We deliberately exclude skin temperature from the primary modality set. On PMED the stimulus is thermal, so skin temperature would leak the experimental manipulation rather than measuring the physiological pain response, and it would not transfer to PMCD, where pain is mechanically induced during physiotherapy.

\paragraph{LLM text-serialised dataset (v2).} For the LLM-based experiments, we additionally produce a text-serialised variant of the same windows, referred to as the v2 dataset. Each window is rendered as structured text with (i) subject-level z-scored features -- including HRV (RMSSD), SCR count, pulse rate, EMG burst count, and respiration-rate variability in addition to the basic statistics, and (ii) a chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning target ending in \texttt{Classification: \{No-pain, Moderate, Severe\}}. An earlier v1 variant used global feature discretisation and a bare single-word target; v2 replaces this with subject-relative normalisation, a richer feature set, and CoT targets. This is the dataset used by all LLM runs reported in this paper.

\paragraph{Preprocessing.}
For the signal-level models, all signals are z-score normalised per modality using training-set statistics (mean and standard deviation computed over all training windows and time steps). Normalisation parameters are computed on the training fold and applied to the test fold in each leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) split, preventing leakage. For classical baselines (RF, SVM), a \texttt{StandardScaler} is applied inside the cross-validation pipeline. For the CrossMod-Transformer, we use per-modality \texttt{RobustScaler} fit on the training fold, which preserves subject-level amplitude cues that carry pain information.

\section{Methodology}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

We formulate automated pain recognition as a subject-independent time-series classification problem using a unified three-class scheme (no-pain / moderate / severe) across both subsets. For PMED, we additionally report binary results (no pain vs.\ high pain) as an upper-bound reference under clean label conditions.

Given a windowed multichannel input $\mathbf{X} = [\mathbf{x}^{(1)}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}^{(M)}]$ where $\mathbf{x}^{(m)} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_m}$ is the signal from modality $m$, the goal is to predict a pain label $y \in \{0, 1, \ldots, K-1\}$.

\subsection{Early-Fusion Baseline}

As a neural baseline, we concatenate all physiological channels along the feature dimension and process them with a single temporal backbone:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_{\text{concat}} = [\mathbf{x}^{(1)} \| \mathbf{x}^{(2)} \| \cdots \| \mathbf{x}^{(M)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times D}, \quad D = \textstyle\sum_{m} d_m
\end{equation}
The backbone consists of stacked 1D convolutional blocks with batch normalisation, ReLU activation, and dropout, followed by global average pooling and a classification head.

\subsection{Attention-Fusion Model}

This model processes each physiological modality through a dedicated temporal encoder before fusing representations via learned attention.

\paragraph{Modality-specific encoders.}
Each channel $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}$ is processed by an independent temporal encoder $f_{\theta_m}$ consisting of stacked 1D convolutional blocks with batch normalization, ReLU, and dropout. Each encoder ends with global average pooling (\texttt{AdaptiveAvgPool1d}), which collapses the
time dimension to a fixed-length vector regardless of input length. This makes encoders agnostic to window duration, enabling the same weights to process both PMED (2{,}500 timesteps) and PMCD (1{,}000 timesteps) without modification. The pooled output is then projected to a shared latent space:
  \begin{equation}
      \mathbf{h}^{(m)}
      = \mathbf{W}_m^{\text{proj}}\, f_{\theta_m}(\mathbf{x}^{(m)})
      \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
  \end{equation}
  where $d$ is a shared latent dimension.

\paragraph{Attention-based fusion.}
We compute attention weights over modality representations $\{\mathbf{h}^{(1)}, \ldots, \mathbf{h}^{(M)}\}$:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_m = \frac{\exp\!\bigl(\mathbf{w}^\top \tanh(\mathbf{W}_a \mathbf{h}^{(m)} + \mathbf{b}_a)\bigr)}{\sum_{j=1}^{M} \exp\!\bigl(\mathbf{w}^\top \tanh(\mathbf{W}_a \mathbf{h}^{(j)} + \mathbf{b}_a)\bigr)}, \qquad
    \mathbf{z} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \alpha_m \, \mathbf{h}^{(m)}
\end{equation}
The fused representation $\mathbf{z}$ is passed through a two-layer MLP with ReLU and dropout:
$\hat{y} = \mathrm{softmax}(\mathrm{MLP}(\mathbf{z}))$.
The learned weights $\alpha_m$ provide an interpretable measure of per-modality importance. We further use an ``Improved Attention-Fusion'' variant with multi-scale kernels (3 / 7 / 15 / 31), temporal attention pooling, InstanceNorm, label smoothing $0.1$, and (on PMCD) focal loss + class-balanced sampling. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows the full attention-fusion pipeline.

\subsection{CrossMod-Transformer}
\label{sec:crossmod}

We further adapt the CrossMod-Transformer of \citet{farmani2025crossmod}, originally designed for EDA + ECG fusion, to the four-modality PainMonit setting. Each modality is processed in parallel by two branches: (i) an FCN with three Conv1D blocks of widths $128 \to 256 \to 128$ and kernels $7 / 5 / 3$, and (ii) an ALSTM consisting of two stacked LSTM-64 layers with Bahdanau attention and an ELiSH activation. For each modality, two intra-modal \texttt{TransformerEncoder} blocks (8 heads, $d_{\text{model}}=128$) plus bidirectional cross-attention fuse the FCN and ALSTM token streams into a single modality embedding $z_m \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$. The $M$ modality embeddings are augmented with a learnable modality embedding and passed through an inter-modal Transformer encoder (2 layers, 8 heads, $d=128$), followed by mean-pooling and a four-layer MLP classifier ($512 \to 256 \to 128 \to K$). The complete model contains $\approx 4.6$\,M parameters, roughly $1{,}000\times$ smaller than the Qwen3-4B LLM. Training uses AdamW + cosine decay with 5-epoch warmup, gradient clipping, per-modality \texttt{RobustScaler}, and auto inverse-frequency class weights on the 4 shared modalities.

\subsection{Cross-Context Transfer Learning}
To test whether PMED representations carry over to PMCD, we first train the attention-fusion model on PMED binary classification, then transfer the modality encoders, projection layers, and attention weights to PMCD and replace the final classification head. We compare two fine-tuning strategies: \emph{frozen encoder} (only the head and fusion layer are updated) and \emph{full fine-tuning} (all parameters are updated, with encoder LRs reduced $10\times$ relative to the head). Both are evaluated against a from-scratch PMCD baseline. For reference we also report LLM-based PMED$\to$PMCD transfer using the clf\_head setup (Section~\ref{sec:llm}).

\subsection{LLM-based Methods}
\label{sec:llm}

We evaluate four LLM-based setups on the v2 text-serialised dataset.

\paragraph{Zero- and few-shot prompting.} An instruction-tuned LLM is queried via API with a structured prompt containing the serialised features and $k \in \{0, 3, 5\}$ demonstrations per class.

\paragraph{Generative SFT with CoT.} Qwen3-4B-Instruct is LoRA fine-tuned on the v2 CoT target (a chain of reasoning ending in \texttt{Classification: <label>}) with the two-stage schedule ``PMED first, then resume on PMCD''. We also run Phi-4-mini (3.8B) with the same pipeline and modified LoRA targets (\texttt{qkv\_proj, o\_proj}).

\paragraph{Reinforcement learning (GRPO / SRPO).} On top of the SFT checkpoint, we run GRPO (exact-match reward: $+1.0$ correct, $0.0$ wrong, $-0.5$ parse failure; group size $G=4$) and SRPO, which additionally distinguishes adjacent-class errors ($-0.3$) from cross-class errors ($-1.0$), with LR $5\text{e-}6$ and temperature $0.7$. Both operate on the generative output.

\paragraph{Classification-head (clf\_head).} We replace the generative head with a frozen backbone plus LoRA ($r{=}8$, $\alpha{=}16$, targets \texttt{q\_proj, v\_proj}) and a linear classification head. The last non-padding hidden state $h_{\text{last}}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ is fed to a linear layer $\mathbb{R}^{d}\to\mathbb{R}^{K}$ and trained with class-weighted cross-entropy. Only the LoRA adapters and the linear head are updated -- about $0.06\%$ of the 4B backbone parameters. This setup removes output-parsing failures by construction and produces calibrated probabilities. We evaluate it on both Qwen3-4B-Instruct and Phi-4-mini.

\subsection{Loss Function}

Let $p_{\theta}(y=k \mid \mathbf{X})$ denote the model's predicted probability of class $k$ for an input window $\mathbf{X}$, and $y^\star \in \{0,\dots,K{-}1\}$ the ground-truth class. The default training loss is class-weighted cross-entropy
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}(\theta) \;=\; -\,\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_{y_i^\star}\,\log p_{\theta}\!\left(y = y_i^\star \mid \mathbf{X}_i\right),
\end{equation}
with class weights $w_k$ set to the inverse class frequency on the training fold. For the LLM classification head on PMCD we use fixed weights $\{1.06, 0.65, 1.94\}$ for No-pain / Moderate / Severe.

Because PMCD is heavily class-imbalanced (485 Severe vs.\ 1443 No-pain), we also train the attention-fusion model with focal loss~\citep{lin2017focal}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}}(\theta) \;=\; -\,\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_{y_i^\star}\,\bigl(1 - p_{t,i}\bigr)^{\gamma}\,\log p_{t,i},
    \qquad p_{t,i} = p_{\theta}\!\left(y = y_i^\star \mid \mathbf{X}_i\right),
\end{equation}
with $\gamma = 2$ following \citet{lin2017focal}. The modulating factor $(1-p_t)^{\gamma}$ down-weights easy examples so that gradient mass flows mainly from hard, under-represented windows.

\paragraph{Classical feature-based models.}
We extract 16 statistical, temporal, and spectral features per sensor (mean, standard deviation, range, spectral entropy, dominant frequency, etc.) and train Random Forest~\citep{breiman2001random}, SVM, and Logistic Regression classifiers. \subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

We report balanced accuracy, macro F1, macro AUC, and per-class precision / recall. The first two matter most because PMCD is class-imbalanced: plain accuracy is dominated by the majority classes. For the signal-level models (RF, attention-fusion, CrossMod-Transformer) we use leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) cross-validation following~\citet{gouverneur2024painmonit} to prevent subject leakage, or grouped 5-fold CV as a faster proxy for the Transformer-scale CrossMod runs. For the LLM runs we use a 10-subject held-out validation split (subject-grouped) because full LOSO on a 4B-parameter backbone is computationally out of reach. All signal-level runs use the same 4 shared modalities (BVP, EDA\_E4, EMG, Resp).

\paragraph{Metric definitions.}
Given $K$ classes, per-class precision, recall, and F1 are
\begin{equation}
    P_k = \frac{\mathrm{TP}_k}{\mathrm{TP}_k + \mathrm{FP}_k}, \quad
    R_k = \frac{\mathrm{TP}_k}{\mathrm{TP}_k + \mathrm{FN}_k}, \quad
    F1_k = \frac{2\,P_k R_k}{P_k + R_k}.
\end{equation}
Macro F1 and balanced accuracy average these per-class scores uniformly:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{F1}_{\text{macro}} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} F1_k, \qquad
    \mathrm{BalAcc} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} R_k.
\end{equation}
For multi-class AUC we use the one-vs-rest macro variant: for each class $k$ we compute the binary ROC AUC of $p_{\theta}(y=k\mid\mathbf{X})$ against the indicator $\mathbf{1}[y^\star = k]$, and average across $k$.

We look at four diagnostic dimensions when reading the results: modality (which channels help), pain context (PMED vs.\ PMCD), label quality (clean CoVAS vs.\ noisy NRS), and class balance (balanced vs.\ skewed PMCD).

\subsection{Implementation Details}
Signals are z-score (or \texttt{RobustScaler}) normalised per modality using training-fold statistics, as described in Section~\ref{sec:dataset}. Windows are extracted following the dataset segmentation protocol (stimulus-aligned 10-second epochs for PMED; 4-second windows with 50\% overlap for PMCD). Neural models are trained with Adam / AdamW~\citep{kingma2015adam} with cosine annealing, batch size 64, gradient clipping, and 5-epoch warmup. For transfer learning, pretrained encoder learning rates are reduced by $10\times$ relative to the classification head. For LLM SFT we use LoRA rank $= 8$, $\alpha = 16$, targets $\{q\_proj, v\_proj\}$ (or $\{qkv\_proj, o\_proj\}$ for Phi-4-mini), batch size $2$ with gradient accumulation $4$, learning rate $10^{-4}$ (PMED) / $5\cdot 10^{-5}$ (PMCD). For clf\_head we use LoRA $r=8$, $\alpha=16$, learning rate $2\cdot10^{-4}$, 10 epochs, cosine schedule.

\subsection{Error Analysis: Two Models, Opposite Failure Modes}
\label{sec:error_analysis}

The two winners fail on PMCD in mirrored ways. CrossMod-Transformer collapses Severe into Moderate: $260/485$ Severe windows ($54\%$) are predicted as Moderate, and Severe recall sits at only $0.23$. Moderate and Severe clinical pain are physiologically close along BVP, EDA, and respiration features; CrossMod's small-parameter specialised fusion amplifies this confusion rather than resisting it, even with inverse-frequency class weights of $2.4\times$ on Severe.

Qwen3-4B with a classification head does the opposite: it over-triggers Severe. On the PMCD validation split, $43\%$ of Moderate windows and $33\%$ of No-pain windows are flagged as Severe. Severe recall jumps to $0.74$ but Moderate precision drops in return. The LLM's prior is too eager to fire on the high-pain class instead of defaulting to Moderate.

The two failure modes are symmetric, which means a single class-imbalance fix will not help both models. Focal loss and inverse-frequency weights are the right dial for CrossMod; threshold calibration or temperature scaling on the softmax are the right dial for the LLM.

\subsection{LLM Ablations}

\paragraph{LLM pipeline baseline.} As an internal starting point for the LLM-based methods, we first ran a minimal Qwen3-4B + LoRA recipe on the v1 dataset with a single-word target (no CoT, no classification head, no subject-level normalisation). This baseline reaches Macro F1 = 0.378 on PMED, 0.474 on PMCD, and 0.436 on the PMED$\to$PMCD transfer setting. The headline clf\_head pipeline reported in this paper (v2 dataset, subject-level z-scored features, CoT target during SFT, frozen backbone with linear classification head) improves PMED Macro F1 by $+0.27$ ($0.378\!\to\!0.649$) and accuracy by $+0.48$ ($0.378\!\to\!0.858$), while PMCD stays roughly flat ($0.474\!\to\!0.464$) and PMED$\to$PMCD transfer drops slightly ($0.436\!\to\!0.418$, but recall improves). The dataset-variant paragraph below isolates the v1$\to$v2 contribution under the clf\_head pipeline; the comparison here additionally folds in the move from generative output to the classification head.

\paragraph{Training-method ablation (same Qwen3-4B backbone).} On PMCD, the classification head outperforms every generative variant: SFT 0.359, GRPO 0.355, SRPO 0.378, clf\_head \textbf{0.464}. GRPO and SRPO recover the No-pain / Severe recall that SFT loses, but they do not close the macro-F1 gap --- generation introduces parsing failures (up to $\approx$1\% of windows on SRPO) and precludes calibrated probabilities. The classification head eliminates both issues by construction.

\paragraph{Backbone ablation (same clf\_head pipeline).} Replacing Qwen3-4B with Phi-4-mini (3.8B) under an otherwise identical pipeline causes PMCD F1 to collapse from 0.464 to 0.252; the Phi-4 model predicts only Moderate on PMCD. On PMED the two backbones reach similar AUC (0.94 vs. 0.94), but Phi-4-mini's Moderate recall collapses to $0.00$, making Qwen3-4B the more stable choice in the 4B tier. PMED$\to$PMCD transfer is also near-random for Phi-4-mini (F1 = 0.131, AUC $\approx 0.50$), vs. 0.418 / 0.623 for Qwen3-4B.

\paragraph{Dataset variant (v1 vs. v2).} We compared the original v1 formulation (global discretisation, basic features, single-word target) against v2 (subject-level z-scores, extended features, CoT target) on the same Qwen3-4B + clf\_head pipeline. On PMED, Macro F1 improves from 0.33 to 0.65; on PMCD the two variants are nearly tied (0.458 vs.\ 0.464). Since v2 changes normalisation, feature set, PMED label derivation, windowing, and output target at the same time, this comparison is not a controlled ablation and should be read as a change-list rather than a single-knob study.

\section{Discussion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Why Does the Winning Architecture Depend on Context?}

No single model wins both subsets: CrossMod-Transformer beats the 4B-parameter LLM on PMCD, and the LLM beats CrossMod by $+0.23$ F1 on PMED. The two subsets ask for different kinds of inductive bias.

On clinical PMCD, pain episodes are heterogeneous (pressure, movement, electrical, or mixed stimuli), labels are sparse and noisy (NRS self-reports mapped to 4-second windows), and BVP / EDA / Resp features carry real pain-related temporal structure across sessions. CrossMod's per-modality FCN + ALSTM encoders and bidirectional cross-attention match this kind of short-window cross-modal structure well, and at 4.6M parameters the model is small enough to train to convergence on the PMCD training set without overfitting. The main ceiling is the $\sim$14\% Severe prevalence, which holds macro F1 near 0.59 even with inverse-frequency weights.

On experimental PMED, the dominant variance is inter-subject. The same heat stimulus produces very different BVP, EDA, and EMG responses across participants, and under subject-held-out evaluation the test subject's amplitude profile is unseen. A 4.6M-parameter specialised network does not have enough prior to generalise across these folds. The LLM backbone does, and the classification head taps those priors without having to generate well-formed text. One might suspect the gap is just a normalisation artefact, but switching CrossMod to per-sample \texttt{RobustScaler} (which would strip out subject-level amplitude) did not recover F1. The mismatch is in the shape and timing of the response, not just its scale.

\subsection{Why Is Cross-Context Transfer Hard?}

PMED$\to$PMCD transfer is worse than in-domain training for every method we tried. RF drops to $F1 = 0.366$ (essentially chance), and the LLM classification head loses $\approx 0.05$ F1 from in-domain ($0.464 \to 0.418$). Heat-pain physiology in healthy subjects and mechanical physiotherapy pain in patients share some features, but the sensor statistics, the label semantics (CoVAS vs.\ NRS), and the baseline physiological state of the participants are all different. The LLM transfers more gracefully than RF, which fits the intuition that large-scale priors are less brittle to distribution shift, but the gap is still large enough that in-domain training is always preferable when it is available.

\subsection{Classification Head vs.\ Generative SFT}

Across training-method variants on the same Qwen3-4B backbone, the classification head is the most reliable setup for small-data, imbalanced physiological classification. It removes output-parsing failures, produces calibrated probabilities (so AUC is meaningful), reaches higher Macro F1 than SFT / GRPO / SRPO on every metric we tracked, and trains roughly $10\times$ faster at inference. This echoes the finding in other tabular-LLM work~\citep{hegselmann2023tabllm}: when the input does not need to be reasoned about in open text, generation adds noise and no upside.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our experiments have several limitations. (i) PMED and PMCD do not share identical modality sets, which caps transfer for non-shared channels. (ii) The dataset contains only 101 participants, so the point estimates are sensitive to subject-level variability even under LOSO. (iii) For compute reasons the LLM runs use a 10-subject held-out validation split rather than full LOSO. This is consistent across LLM variants but not directly comparable to the LOSO numbers for the signal-level models; we do not expect the ranking to change, but the absolute F1 values might move a few points. (iv) The LLM classification head works on text-serialised features, not raw waveforms, which loses the fine-grained temporal information a time-series encoder would see.

%% ============================================================
\section{Future Work}
%% ============================================================

Three directions follow directly from our analysis. (1) Architecture-specific class-imbalance fixes: focal loss plus oversampling for CrossMod's Severe collapse, and threshold calibration or softmax temperature scaling for the LLM's Severe over-triggering. (2) Larger or domain-specific LLM backbones: swapping Qwen3-4B for Qwen3-8B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, or a medical-pretrained model in the same clf\_head pipeline, to see whether PMCD in-domain performance can catch up to CrossMod. (3) Repeating the clinical-vs-experimental comparison on BioVid and X-ITE to check whether the architecture--context interaction we observe is specific to PainMonit or a broader property of physiological pain datasets.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We benchmarked six model families on the PainMonit Database (PMED and PMCD) under a single subject-independent protocol. The headline finding is that the best model depends on the subset. CrossMod-Transformer wins on clinical PMCD (Macro F1 = 0.594) with roughly $1{,}000\times$ fewer parameters than the LLM, while a Qwen3-4B classification head wins on experimental PMED (Macro F1 = 0.649, AUC = 0.940). Cross-context transfer drops every method, but the LLM drops the least. Within the LLM family, a frozen backbone with LoRA and a linear classification head beats generative SFT, GRPO, and SRPO on the same backbone, and the backbone itself matters: Phi-4-mini collapses on PMCD under an otherwise identical pipeline. A practical reading of these numbers is that specialised fusion is still the right tool for in-domain clinical data, while LLM priors are the right tool when inter-subject variability is the dominant source of noise, and that the two should probably be combined rather than chosen.
